Overview

Hysterectomy at OSHUNJA offers surgical removal of the uterus to treat conditions unresponsive to conservative therapy‚Äîincluding fibroids causing severe bleeding or pain, endometriosis, uterine prolapse, gynecological cancers, or chronic pelvic pain. Our Kingston surgeons specialize in minimally invasive approaches (laparoscopic and vaginal) that minimize scarring, reduce recovery time, and optimize outcomes compared to traditional open surgery.

This permanent procedure eliminates menstruation and prevents pregnancy, making it appropriate only after completing childbearing or when fertility preservation isn't desired. Depending on your condition, we may remove just your uterus (partial hysterectomy), uterus plus cervix (total hysterectomy), or include ovaries and fallopian tubes (complete hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy). Each approach is tailored to your specific medical needs.

While hysterectomy is major surgery, advances in minimally invasive techniques mean most women return home within 24 hours and resume normal activities within 2-4 weeks. Our experienced surgical team combines technical excellence with compassionate care, ensuring you understand every aspect of your procedure and feel confident in your decision to pursue definitive treatment.

How It Works & What To Expect

1

Comprehensive Evaluation

Consultation includes medical history review, pelvic examination, imaging (ultrasound or MRI), and discussion of surgical approach. We confirm conservative treatments have been exhausted and that you understand the permanent nature of hysterectomy.

2

Pre-Surgical Preparation

Pre-operative testing (blood work, ECG) ensures safety. You'll receive detailed instructions about medications, fasting, and what to bring. Bowel prep may be required depending on surgical approach.

3

Surgery

General anesthesia is administered. Depending on your condition, we perform vaginal, laparoscopic, or abdominal hysterectomy. The uterus (and sometimes cervix, ovaries, or fallopian tubes) is removed. Minimally invasive approaches when possible reduce scarring and speed recovery.

4

Hospital Stay & Initial Recovery

Vaginal and laparoscopic hysterectomies typically allow same-day or overnight discharge. Abdominal hysterectomy may require 1-2 days hospitalization. Pain management and early mobilization begin immediately.

5

Follow-Up & Long-Term Care

Post-operative visits monitor healing and address concerns. Most women return to normal activities within 4-6 weeks. If ovaries were preserved, no hormone replacement needed. If removed, we discuss hormone therapy options.

Ideal Candidates & Considerations

Hysterectomy May Be Right for You If:

  • You have large or numerous fibroids causing severe pain, heavy bleeding, or urinary symptoms unresponsive to conservative treatment
  • Endometriosis hasn't improved with medication or surgery, causing chronic debilitating pelvic pain
  • You experience abnormal uterine bleeding uncontrolled by medications or endometrial ablation
  • You've been diagnosed with uterine, cervical, or ovarian cancer requiring surgical treatment
  • Severe uterine prolapse significantly impacts quality of life and conservative management failed
  • You've completed childbearing and understand pregnancy will be impossible after hysterectomy
  • Conservative treatments (medications, minimally invasive procedures) haven't provided adequate relief

Important Considerations:

  • Permanent Sterility: You can never become pregnant after hysterectomy‚Äîensure childbearing is complete
  • Hormone Changes: If ovaries are removed, surgical menopause begins immediately. Hormone replacement therapy may be needed
  • Recovery Time: Plan for 4-6 weeks off work and restricted activities‚Äîlonger for abdominal approach
  • Surgical Approach: Vaginal and laparoscopic methods offer faster recovery when feasible; abdominal may be necessary for large uteruses
  • Alternatives: Ensure you've explored all conservative options first unless cancer is present
  • Psychological Impact: Some women experience grief or loss; counseling support available if needed
Important: Hysterectomy is major surgery with permanent consequences. We ensure this is the right choice through comprehensive evaluation and discussion of all alternatives.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will I go through menopause after hysterectomy?

Only if your ovaries are also removed. If ovaries remain, they continue producing hormones and you won't experience menopause symptoms. If ovaries are removed, surgical menopause begins immediately and hormone replacement therapy may be recommended.

How long is recovery from hysterectomy?

Vaginal and laparoscopic hysterectomies typically require 2-4 weeks before returning to work and 6 weeks for full recovery. Abdominal hysterectomy may need 4-6 weeks before work and 6-12 weeks for complete healing. Recovery varies by individual.

Will my sex life change after hysterectomy?

Most women experience no negative changes and many report improved sexual function after relief from pain and bleeding. The vagina remains intact and sensation is preserved. If ovaries are removed, vaginal dryness may occur but is treatable with hormone therapy or lubricants.

Should I keep my ovaries or have them removed?

This decision depends on your age, cancer risk, and personal preferences. Ovary preservation avoids surgical menopause. Removal may be recommended if you have ovarian disease, strong family history of ovarian cancer, or are postmenopausal. We'll discuss your specific situation thoroughly.

Will I gain weight after hysterectomy?

Hysterectomy itself doesn't cause weight gain. Some women gain weight during recovery due to decreased activity. If ovaries are removed, hormonal changes may affect metabolism. Maintaining healthy diet and resuming exercise after recovery prevents weight gain.

Do I still need Pap smears after hysterectomy?

If your cervix was removed (total hysterectomy) and you have no history of cervical cancer or precancerous changes, Pap smears are generally not needed. If cervix remains (subtotal hysterectomy), continue regular Pap smears. Annual well-woman exams remain important regardless.

Is laparoscopic hysterectomy better than abdominal?

Laparoscopic and vaginal approaches offer faster recovery, less pain, shorter hospital stays, and smaller scars when medically feasible. However, very large uteruses, extensive scarring, or cancer may require abdominal approach for safety. Your surgeon will recommend the best option for your situation.

What are the risks of hysterectomy?

Like all major surgery, risks include bleeding, infection, blood clots, anesthesia complications, and injury to nearby organs (bladder, bowel, ureters). Serious complications are rare with experienced surgeons. We take extensive precautions to minimize all risks.

Can I have hysterectomy if I have a history of C-sections?

Yes. Previous cesarean deliveries may increase surgical complexity due to scar tissue but don't prevent hysterectomy. Your surgeon will review your surgical history and may recommend specific approaches to ensure safety.

Is hysterectomy covered by insurance?

When medically necessary for conditions like fibroids, endometriosis, cancer, or abnormal bleeding, hysterectomy is typically covered by insurance. Coverage varies by plan. Our team assists with pre-authorization and insurance coordination.
